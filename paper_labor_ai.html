Working paper · Labor / AI exposure

Rising Manager Skills under AI: Scarcity Premium or Complementarity?

Skill Matrix project — Marketing, HR, and Financial Managers

Lightcast job postings, 2023–2026 · standardized skills (≥5% either year) × task-level AI exposure

Abstract

When skills become more common in manager job postings, that rise is often treated as a single AI story. We argue it is not. Combining 2023→2026 skill penetration in Lightcast postings with a task-level AI-exposure score, we decompose rising demand into two economically distinct channels: a human premium (rising + low exposure: scarcity of hard-to-automate work) and an AI-transformed channel (rising + higher exposure: complementarity / expansion around automatable production). Across Marketing, HR, and Financial Managers, rising demand is dominated by the premium channel, but a non-trivial transformed share shows that AI also expands demand for AI-adjacent managerial work. Falling high-exposure skills identify a displacement residual; falling low-exposure skills are largely a non-AI residual.

1. Introduction

Public debate after large language models often collapses into a binary: AI replaces routine work; humans keep “soft skills.” For managerial occupations that binary is too coarse. Manager postings ask for judgment, coordination, analytics, communication, and increasingly for the ability to use AI tools. Empirically, many skills rise in posting share between 2023 and 2026 — including some that AI can partly perform.

This paper asks a labor question with curricular bite: when manager skills rise in demand, do they look more like scarcity (a human premium) or like complementarity with AI (transformed demand)? We answer with an empirical decomposition, not a causal hiring model.

2. Conceptual framework

Cross skill demand change with AI exposure:

  • Human premium — rising demand, low exposure: scarce human bottlenecks.
  • AI-transformed — rising demand, higher exposure: complementarity / Jevons-like expansion (more output when production cheapens).
  • Displacement — falling demand, higher exposure: substitution residual.
  • Non-AI shift — falling demand, low exposure: residual not primarily about AI.

Default exposure threshold = 0.5 (same as the dashboard Mechanisms tab). Rising = non-negative change in penetration (percentage points).

3. Data and measurement

We use Lightcast postings for three O*NET manager occupations — Marketing Managers (11-2021.00), Human Resources Managers (11-3121.00), and Financial Managers (11-3031.00) — in 2023 and 2026. The analysis unit is the standardized Lightcast skill. We keep skills with ≥5% penetration in 2023 or 2026 (plus a noise filter). Penetration is the share of postings mentioning the skill at least once.

AI exposure is scored on each skill label with a transparent keyword rubric (neutral base 0.5 ± HIGH/LOW keyword hits). Scoring is matrix-independent — the AI Skill Matrix is not used as a prior on skills. This covers the full ≥5% universe.

5. Discussion

The main labor fact is compositional: rising manager skill demand is mostly a human-premium story, consistent with managerial work overweighting judgment, influence, and coordination. Yet the transformed share is not zero — AI also expands demand for skills adjacent to automation (orchestrating, verifying, applying tools). Treating “skills that rise” as synonymous with “soft skills AI cannot touch” misreads the second channel.

Displacement exists as a residual among falling high-exposure skills, but in these management occupations it is not the dominant mass of the distribution. Falling low-exposure skills remind us that not every decline is an AI story.

6. Limitations

  • Two time points only — correlational description, not an event study or causal identification.
  • Skills below 5% in both years are excluded by design (stated universe floor).
  • Exposure scores are rubric-based, not firm-validated task measurements.
  • Posting volume grew sharply 2023→2026; composition and collection changes may confound raw counts (we use penetration shares).
  • Management occupations likely over-represent the premium channel relative to more routine occupations.

7. Conclusion

Rising skill demand in manager postings has two AI faces. Most of the rise looks like scarcity of hard-to-automate human work; a smaller but meaningful share looks like complementarity with AI. Counting those channels — rather than narrating a single “AI skills” trend — is the contribution of this paper.
